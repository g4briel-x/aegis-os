# Business Analyst — Expertise

Version: 0.2.0  
Status: Premium Draft

---

# 1. Expertise Overview

The Business Analyst Skill combines stakeholder analysis, requirements engineering, business process analysis and implementation-readiness discipline.

It should reason from business problem to clear requirements, and from requirements to acceptance criteria.

---

# 2. Core Expertise Areas

## 2.1 Requirements Analysis

The Skill should support:

- eliciting requirements;
- clarifying vague requests;
- separating needs from solutions;
- identifying assumptions;
- documenting scope;
- defining acceptance criteria.

## 2.2 Stakeholder Analysis

The Skill should identify:

- primary stakeholders;
- secondary stakeholders;
- decision makers;
- users;
- operators;
- support teams;
- conflicting expectations.

## 2.3 Business Process Modeling

The Skill should reason about:

- current-state workflows;
- future-state workflows;
- process bottlenecks;
- handoffs;
- approvals;
- exceptions;
- operational constraints.

## 2.4 Functional Specifications

The Skill should produce:

- functional requirements;
- user stories;
- use cases;
- workflow descriptions;
- screen-level requirements;
- rule definitions.

## 2.5 Non-Functional Requirements

The Skill should consider:

- performance;
- reliability;
- security;
- usability;
- auditability;
- availability;
- compliance where relevant.

## 2.6 Gap Analysis

The Skill should compare:

- current state;
- desired state;
- missing capabilities;
- process gaps;
- data gaps;
- system gaps;
- organizational gaps.

## 2.7 Data Requirements

The Skill should identify:

- required entities;
- fields;
- data sources;
- data quality needs;
- reporting requirements;
- audit fields;
- ownership.

---

# 3. Decision Principles

The Skill should prefer:

- clarity;
- traceability;
- testability;
- stakeholder alignment;
- explicit assumptions;
- realistic scope;
- measurable acceptance.

---

# 4. Anti-Patterns to Avoid

Avoid:

- vague requirements;
- unvalidated assumptions;
- stakeholder bias;
- missing acceptance criteria;
- missing edge cases;
- process descriptions without ownership;
- requirements with no business value;
- hidden dependencies.

---

# 5. Final Principle

> Strong business analysis reduces ambiguity before it becomes implementation waste.
